Methodology

Basic Assumptions

  1. No matter how much your income increases, you'll still spend the same each year to maximize your savings. Example: If you're living on $30k and your income increases, you'll still live on $30k and add the extra money to your savings
  2. When you retire, you'll live off of the dividends from your savings portfolio

Technical Assumptions

  1. Income taxes
    1. Included in calculations:
      - 2020 marginal tax brackets applicable to each province and include EI/CPP premiums
      - Basic Personal Amount tax credit applicable to each province
    2. Excluded in calculations:
      - Other tax deductions or credits that may be applied to your specific situation (ex: Canada Child Benefit, RRSP coontributions, etc.)
  2. Income growth
    1. This input is based on income growth exceeding inflation. In other words, the increase in purchasing power of your income
    2. The average inflation-adjusted yearly income growth in Canada is approximately 1%
    3. Example: If your inflation-adjusted income growth is 0% then your income will increase at the same rate of inflation and your purchasing power will be unaffected. If your inflation-adjusted income growth is 2% then your income will increase at 2% more than the rate of inflation
  3. Investment return
    1. Assumes that all your savings are invested in a diversified pool of assets and that any dividends received are reinvested
    2. This input reflects the inflation-adjusted average yearly return on the overall market
    3. For reference the average inflation-adjusted return on the S&P 500 is about 7%
  4. Retirement
    1. Assumes that all your savings are sold at retirement and are reinvested into a high divided portfolio
    2. Capital Gains tax of 50% is included in this transaction
    3. Assumes a conservative 3% after-tax dividend yield on the portfolio
